% Abstract

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) are rarely single anomalous events. They unfold as causal event chains spanning initial compromise, persistence, lateral movement, and exfiltration. What a security analyst ultimately needs is therefore not a suspicious time window or a set of suspicious nodes, but a chain-shaped narrative explaining how the attack happened. Existing provenance-based intrusion detection systems, however, largely report anomalies at the window, entity, or edge level; the attack chain is reconstructed only after alerts are raised, making investigation vulnerable to dependency explosion and upstream missed detections. This paper presents a chain-level APT detection method that first asks which events form structurally credible causal chains, and then asks whether those chains deviate from benign system behavior. Under an explicit kernel-audit semantics, we define a chain coherence score $\branchCoherence$ that tightens provenance reachability using observable evidence such as process identity continuity, resource-state survival, and finite identity scope. The score guides extraction of candidate causal chains from large audit graphs. A self-supervised sequence model, trained only on benign chains, then assigns semantic anomaly scores to the extracted chains. This design separates chain extraction from maliciousness judgment: $\branchCoherence$ establishes structural plausibility, while the anomaly score $A(\chain)$ drives alerts. We evaluate the system on DARPA Transparent Computing data, covering coherence validation, end-to-end detection, ablations, cross-engagement generalization, and efficiency. The results show that chain-level modeling produces outputs closer to attack narratives and substantially reduces analyst investigation cost relative to traditional window- or node-level alerts.
